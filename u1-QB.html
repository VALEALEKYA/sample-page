🎯 Core Concepts

1. What is Data Science?

Data Science is the interdisciplinary field that acts as a bridge between raw data and actionable business decisions. For an engineer, it is the intersection of:

  • Computer Science: Programming (Python/R) and databases to handle large-scale data.
  • Mathematics & Statistics: Calculus, Linear Algebra, and Probability to understand why algorithms work.
  • Domain Expertise: Understanding the actual business problem to ensure insights are useful.

In short: It is the art of extracting meaningful, predictive insights from noisy, structured, and unstructured data.

2. What is Data Science in the context of modern engineering?
Data science is an interdisciplinary field that uses scientific methods, processes, algorithms, and systems to extract knowledge and insights from noisy, structured, and unstructured data. For an engineer, it's the toolkit used to apply actionable insights from data across a broad range of application domains.
3. What does "Statistical Thinking in the Age of Big Data" mean?
It means recognizing that all work occurs in interconnected processes and that variation exists everywhere. In the era of Big Data, statistical thinking is the mindset required to understand this variation, avoid biases, and make informed decisions rather than just blindly trusting large volumes of data.

🔄 The Life Cycle

4. Why is "Problem Definition" the most critical step in the Life Cycle?
Without a clear problem definition, you might build a brilliant model that solves the wrong problem. This step aligns the engineering efforts with actual business or research needs, defining the scope, goals, and success metrics before any code is written.
5. Differentiate between Data Cleaning and Data Exploration (EDA).

Data Cleaning is the janitorial work: removing duplicates, handling missing values, and fixing formats.

Data Exploration (EDA) is the detective work: using statistics and visualizations to find patterns, spot anomalies, and form hypotheses about the cleaned data.

📐 Math & Statistics

7. Explain Inferential vs Descriptive Statistics.

Think of statistics as a camera.

📸 Descriptive Statistics (The Photograph): Describes or summarizes the data you currently have. It doesn't guess anything outside of the dataset.
Tools: Mean, Median, Mode, Standard Deviation.
Example: Calculating the average GPA of the 50 students in your classroom.

🔮 Inferential Statistics (The Crystal Ball): Uses a small sample to make predictions or generalizations about a much larger population.
Tools: Hypothesis Testing, ANOVA, T-tests.
Example: Asking 50 students who they will vote for, and using that to predict the national election.

8. What is ANOVA and why is it used?
Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is a statistical formula used to compare variances across the means (or average) of different groups. A range of scenarios use it to determine if there is any statistically significant difference between the means of three or more independent (unrelated) groups.
9. Program to find outliers & Box Plot for given sample.

We use the Interquartile Range (IQR) Method. An outlier falls below Q1 - 1.5*IQR or above Q3 + 1.5*IQR.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# The Dataset
sample = [15, 101, 18, 7, 13, 16, 11, 50, 23, 44, 35, 21, 5, 15, 10, 9]

# Step 1: Calculate Q1 and Q3
Q1 = np.percentile(sample, 25)
Q3 = np.percentile(sample, 75)

# Step 2: Calculate IQR
IQR = Q3 - Q1

# Step 3: Define Bounds
lower_bound = Q1 - (1.5 * IQR)
upper_bound = Q3 + (1.5 * IQR)

# Step 4: Identify Outliers (List Comprehension)
outliers = [x for x in sample if x < lower_bound or x > upper_bound]

print(f"Outliers: {outliers}") # Spoiler: 101 is the outlier!

# Step 5: Draw Box Plot
plt.boxplot(sample, vert=False)
plt.title("Box Plot of Sample Data")
plt.show()
